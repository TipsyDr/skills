# Web-Event Adaptation Guide

Use this reference when fiction needs light jokes, public-opinion texture, web-event patterns, public-personality catchphrases, or public-figure incident shadows without creating harmful real-world claims.

## Core Rule

Real web events can be imitated as plot templates, public-opinion structures, and comedy mechanics. Direct catchphrases can be used when short, public, non-defamatory, and scene-compatible. Prefer fictional names and settings unless the user asks for a neutral factual reference.

For currently trending short-video, streamer, or variety-show material, verify public facts when recency matters. Borrow the social mechanism, and use direct phrase references sparingly: opening energy, audience call-and-response, teammate teasing, accidental failure, public remix, or closing banter.

## Reuse Limit

- Count each recognizable event template like a meme.
- Use the same event template at most twice in one book by default.
- Keep repeated uses far apart, preferably across different arcs.
- If a template is needed again, change the social surface and story function, or rewrite it into ordinary character voice.

## Safe Templates

### Public-Relations Flip

Use for factions, companies, academies, or guilds trying to turn failure into virtue.

Pattern:
- Official statement uses clean language.
- Forum users notice what the statement avoids saying.
- A character translates the polite words into plain stakes.

Risk boundary:
- Do not imitate a real organization's wording closely.
- Do not imply a real-world scandal.

### Livestream Failure

Use for public tests, arena scenes, school events, or dungeon broadcasts.

Pattern:
- The official camera misses or cuts a key moment.
- Backup footage, audience clips, or timestamps expose the gap.
- Commenters demand verification.

Risk boundary:
- Keep it fictional and story-specific.
- Avoid linking it to real platform scandals unless asked and fact-checked.

### Prewritten Statement

Use for suspiciously fast allegations, water-army posts, or faction spin.

Pattern:
- Comments repeat identical punctuation, labels, or sentence order.
- A media-savvy character notices the template.
- The protagonist answers with verifiable facts instead of emotion.

Risk boundary:
- Do not accuse real groups.
- Do not make harassment instructions.

### Persona Collapse

Use for arrogant rivals, packaged geniuses, fake experts, or polished officials.

Pattern:
- A character's public image conflicts with live behavior.
- The scene reveals the gap through action, not exposition.
- Public reaction shifts from worship to scrutiny.

Risk boundary:
- Do not map the character too closely to a real celebrity or influencer.
- Avoid real-life gossip references.

### Evidence Check

Use for mystery, trial, public reveal, or scoring disputes.

Pattern:
- Someone demands raw footage, timestamps, original logs, or multiple witnesses.
- The protagonist provides a limited but solid evidence chain.
- The crowd moves from noise to inspection.

Risk boundary:
- Do not turn evidence checking into a full system answer.
- Keep the protagonist's reasoning central.

### Comment-Section Pileup

Use for high-energy public reversals.

Pattern:
- Short comments stack reactions: confusion, proof demand, joke, local knowledge, partial defense.
- Different commenter types sound distinct.
- One plain witness comment can outweigh a dozen clever jokes.

Risk boundary:
- Do not let everyone speak in the same meme voice.
- Do not joke about victims, rescue failures, disease, death, or public-safety harm.

### Streamer or Variety-Show Catchphrase

Use for fictional livestream rooms, school broadcast commentary, teammate banter, or public reaction montages.

Pattern:
- A repeated phrase marks the speaker's role or room energy.
- Other characters remix it under pressure.
- The phrase becomes funny because the situation changes, or because a familiar public catchphrase lands in a new fictional pressure scene.

Safe transformations:
- Real creator greeting -> fictional team entry chant.
- Show-ending catchphrase -> group-chat joke before a dangerous deadline.
- Brothers teasing each other -> teammate trust beat.
- Audience repeats a line -> comment-section pressure or comic relief.

Risk boundary:
- Direct short catchphrases are allowed, but do not copy the real creator's name, exact catchphrase, voice delivery, personal background, and incident context together.
- Do not make the fictional character an obvious one-to-one stand-in for a living person unless the user explicitly asks for parody and the result avoids defamation, harassment, and sensitive real-world claims.
- Count the phrase pattern under the book-level reuse limit.

## Placement Guidance

Best surfaces:
- Danmu and livestream chat.
- Forums and school group chats.
- Bai Zhi-style media commentary.
- Zhou Ye-style pressure-release banter.
- Anonymous route-analysis posts.

Avoid:
- Core battle rules.
- Serious rescue beats.
- Legal/official conclusions.
- Lin Zhou's deepest inner vows.
- Father old-case truth reveals.

## Review Checklist

Before keeping a web-event adaptation, verify:

1. If it imitates a real event, does it avoid inventing harmful claims about real people or organizations?
2. Does it reveal character, social pressure, or public opinion?
3. Would the scene still work if the joke/template were removed?
4. Has this meme, joke structure, or event template already appeared twice in the book?
5. Is it safely away from victim suffering, death, illness, disaster, and public-safety harm?
