# Novel Usage Guide for Chinese Hot Memes

## Purpose

Convert hot memes into usable fiction texture. The goal is not to make every line trendy; the goal is to make scenes feel socially alive when the story surface supports internet language.

## Intensity Levels

| Level | Use case | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Serious chapters, older characters, long-lived prose | Paraphrase the meme meaning without exact slang |
| Medium | Young characters, group chats, comedic dialogue | Use one recognizable meme directly |
| High | Livestreams, danmu, comment sections, satire | Stack multiple short meme reactions |

## Scene Surfaces

| Surface | Best use |
|---|---|
| Dialogue | Show age, platform habit, relationship closeness, teasing, conflict |
| Inner monologue | Light self-mockery, pressure relief, reaction beats |
| Danmu/live comments | Dense, fast, repetitive memes; direct use is acceptable |
| Forum/comment section | Public judgment, group emotion, mock analysis, proof demands |
| Short-video copy | Hook phrases, exaggerated labels, lifestyle tags |
| Chapter title | Use sparingly for comedic arcs or modern web-novel flavor |

## Genre Adaptation

- Urban fantasy / modern high martial: allow direct hot memes in dialogue, danmu, forums, short videos, and school/workplace scenes.
- Xianxia / fantasy: prefer transformed equivalents unless the scene has a modern narrator, system UI, transmigration setup, or audience commentary.
- Suspense / horror: use low intensity. A single casual meme can create contrast, but dense slang weakens dread.
- Romance / slice of life: use lifestyle and self-care terms when they reveal emotional needs.
- Comedy / satire: high intensity is acceptable, especially in public reaction surfaces.

## Character Fit Checklist

Before adding a meme, answer:

1. Would this character know this phrase?
2. Would this character say it in front of this listener?
3. Does the line reveal emotion, relationship, class, region, platform habit, or pressure?
4. If the meme is removed, does the scene still make sense?

If answers 1-3 are weak, paraphrase or remove the meme.

## Reuse Discipline

- Track exact meme phrases, joke structures, and web-event templates at the book level when revising chapters in batches.
- Use the same recognizable meme phrase, joke structure, or event template at most twice in one book by default.
- Keep repeated uses far apart. Avoid reuse in adjacent paragraphs, adjacent scenes, or adjacent chapters unless the repetition is deliberately functioning as a chant, catchphrase, water-army copy, livestream刷屏, or evidence-check motif.
- If a meme or event pattern has already appeared twice, switch to ordinary character voice, a non-meme reaction, or a different template.
- Semantic variety matters. Do not make tiny wording changes just to reuse the same joke again.

## Web-Event and Public-Figure Adaptation

- Real web events and public-figure incidents may be imitated as story templates when the imitation serves plot, public-opinion texture, or comedy mechanics.
- Convert real material into fictional patterns such as `直播翻车`, `公关话术`, `预制爆料`, `反转打脸`, `证据核验`, `剪辑误导`, `人设崩塌`, or `评论区复读`; closer imitation is allowed when it is clearly transformed into the fictional world and does not create harmful claims about real people.
- Public-personality catchphrases can be used directly if they are short, public, non-defamatory, and scene-compatible. They still count under the book-level reuse limit and should not become every character's shared voice.
- If the source is a currently trending short-video creator, streamer, or variety-show line, verify public facts when recency matters. Use direct catchphrases sparingly, and avoid combining real name, real platform identifier, exact incident details, exact delivery, and personality traits into one fictional stand-in.
- Prefer safe scene surfaces: forums, danmu, Bai Zhi's media commentary, Zhou Ye's banter, roadside comments, school group chats, and public-opinion montage.
- Do not place event jokes inside core lore, battle rules, official legal conclusions, serious rescue beats, or Lin Zhou's evidence reasoning.
- Do not name real public figures, real organizations, or identifiable controversy details unless the user explicitly asks for a fact-checked nonfiction reference.
- Never turn disasters, deaths, victims, illness, public-safety incidents, vulnerable groups, or ordinary fear into punchlines.

## Public-Personality Catchphrase Adaptation

- Use the underlying comedic mechanism first; direct catchphrase use is allowed when it is brief, public, non-defamatory, and naturally belongs on the scene surface.
- Safe transformations include:
  - streamer opening phrase -> fictional team rally or livestream entry line;
  - variety-show closing banter -> chapter-end pressure release or group-chat joke;
  - brothers roasting each other -> teammate trust and stress relief;
  - audience remixing a catchphrase -> forum/danmu public reaction;
  - improvised failure becoming a running joke -> one fictional arc-specific callback.
- Avoid making a real creator's exact catchphrase a recurring fictional catchphrase unless there is a deliberate story function and it stays within the reuse limit.
- Do not combine exact phrase, real name, real social platform, real incident, and real personality traits into one fictional stand-in.
- Count public-personality catchphrase patterns under the same reuse limit: at most twice per book by default, with distance between uses.

## Update Discipline

New memes should enter `evolving-hot-memes.md` first. Promote them into a main reference only after they have enough source support or repeated user value in fiction tasks.
